At six in the morning at the end of August, Brancaster beach belongs to about four people. The tide is a long way out, the sand is firm and ridged and goes on for what looks like a mile, and the only sound is oystercatchers and the wind. We had driven up from Cambridge the night before specifically so we could be standing here this early, and it was worth every minute of the alarm.

That is the trick with this stretch of north Norfolk. The beaches are enormous and the villages behind them are small and expensive, and the two things reward completely different approaches. Get up early for the coast. Be relaxed about everything else.

This is a two day weekend based in Burnham Market, with the coast on Saturday and the Sandringham estate on Sunday. It also includes one recommendation to skip something that every other guide will tell you to see.

## Why go

The north Norfolk coast has a scale to it that surprises people who have only seen it in photographs. At low tide the sand at Brancaster runs out so far that the sea becomes a rumour, and the light does something particular over that much wet sand and open sky. Behind the beach are salt marshes threaded with creeks, and behind those are flint villages full of very good food and ample flowing drink.

It is also compact. You can be on an empty beach at seven, at a Roman fort at eleven, eating Brancaster mussels at one and looking at a royal drawing room the following morning, without ever driving more than half an hour.

![Wooden groyne posts standing in shallow water on the wide flat sand at Brancaster beach](/images/brancaster-beach-groyne-posts-low-tide.jpg)

## What people underestimate: the tide

This is the part of the weekend that needs saying properly, because the coast here is genuinely dangerous and it does not look it.

Out beyond the beach at Brancaster sits the wreck of the SS Vina, a cargo ship that ended its life as target practice for the RAF. At low tide it is clearly visible from the sand and it draws people towards it like a magnet. It is separated from the beach by a wide, deep, fast flowing tidal channel, and the sandbanks around it are cut off twice a day.

People are rescued there every year, sometimes in large numbers. There has been at least one death, and a coroner has since asked the government to establish who actually owns the wreck so that proper warning signs can be put up. The National Trust's advice is not "take care", it is do not cross the main channel, at any state of the tide. The RNLI's rule of thumb is to be back on dry land, with no channels left to cross, at least four hours before high tide.

We saw the Vina from the beach in the early morning light, from a long way back, and it was a lovely thing to look at from exactly where we were standing. That is the version of this we would recommend. Check the tide times before you go, read the signs at the beach entrance, and treat the wreck as scenery rather than a destination.

## What to do

### Brancaster beach, early

Go at low tide and go early. The car park on Beach Road is the only realistic option and it is not cheap: we paid £6 for two hours in late summer. It is not owned or managed by the National Trust despite the beach being Trust land, so membership does not get you out of paying. In peak season it fills up, and there is no on street parking to fall back on.

What you get for it is one of the best beaches in England before anyone else is on it. There are toilets by the car park and a kiosk opposite doing coffee and buckets and spades if you fancy a morning dig.

### Branodunum Roman fort and the marsh path

A mile or so east along the coastal road is Branodunum, a Roman shore fort that guarded this coast in the third century. Be clear about what survives: the fort itself is now a grassy field with earthworks rather than standing walls, so if you arrive expecting masonry you will be disappointed. What makes it worth the stop is the setting and the walk.

![A mown path running through long golden summer grass across the site of Branodunum Roman fort](/images/branodunum-roman-fort-brancaster-norfolk.jpg)

From the fort there is a boardwalk loop out over the marsh that takes twenty to thirty minutes at an easy pace. In late August with the sun out it is one of the loveliest short walks on this coast, all creeks and moored boats and samphire. In winter, or after rain, expect it to be wet and muddy underfoot and dress accordingly.

![Salt marsh at Brancaster Staithe with tidal creeks, moored boats and a sandy path in the foreground](/images/brancaster-staithe-marsh-boardwalk-view.jpg)

### Sandringham, on the Sunday

The house, gardens and museum come on one ticket, currently around £22.80 if booked online in advance, which is a few pounds cheaper than turning up on the day. Book ahead anyway, because your ticket carries a timed slot in summer and that slot is your house entry time, not your arrival time. Allow up to half an hour to walk from the car park to the house, which nobody tells you and which is enough to make you late if you have not planned for it.

![The front of Sandringham House in Norfolk, red brick with striped lawns leading up to the entrance](/images/sandringham-house-front-lawn-norfolk.jpg)

Inside, you see eight ground floor rooms, the saloon and drawing room and dining room among them. It takes thirty minutes to an hour. It is beautiful, and it is a comfortable country house rather than a palace, so calibrate accordingly. Photography is not allowed inside.

The museum was the better half for us. Queen Victoria's donkey carriage is the standout, and there are royal vehicles, estate fire engines, and cabinets of gifts and trophies that say more about the family than the drawing room does.

Then give the afternoon to the gardens. Sixty acres, two lakes, and enough benches that you can sit for an hour and feel no urgency at all. King Charles has been restoring parts of the grounds in recent years, so they should be even better now than when we saw them.

One caution on timing: the estate's season runs from late March to early October, with some fixed closures within it for events, and the house can also shut at short notice when the family are in residence. Check the dates before you commit a Sunday to it. The Royal Parkland is free and open all year if the house is shut.

### St Mary Magdalene church

Do not leave without this one. It is inside the estate grounds, twenty minutes is enough, and plenty of visitors walk straight past it. The interior is extraordinary for a parish church: a carved and gilded roof with angels, a silver altar, and memorials to George V and George VI. It was the single most striking interior of our weekend, including the house.

![The richly decorated interior of St Mary Magdalene church at Sandringham, with a gilded roof, silver altar and stained glass](/images/st-mary-magdalene-church-sandringham-interior.jpg)

### Castle Rising

Fifteen minutes from Sandringham and the best value of the weekend. Adults £5.25, children £3.50, a family ticket at £15.75, and under fives free. The castle's own site lists English Heritage members as free, though it is privately owned rather than run by English Heritage, so take your card and be prepared to pay if there is a question at the desk.

![The Norman stone keep at Castle Rising standing on mown grass surrounded by earthworks](/images/castle-rising-castle-keep-norfolk.jpg)

What you get is a twelfth century keep you can properly climb, with stairs, passages and chambers, ringed by huge earthworks. It is remarkably intact for its age and it takes about an hour. The stairs are steep and spiral in places, so it suits able bodies better than tired ones. Note that if you are visiting in the off season of November to March, it only opens Wednesday to Sunday and closes at four.

## The village everyone tells you to visit

Burnham Market gets called Chelsea on Sea, and every guide to this coast will send you there. Here is the honest version: it is a genuinely pretty Georgian village around a green, with expensive independent shops and some very good places to eat, and you can walk the whole of it in well under an hour. If you are in the market for a quilted jacket or a nice bit of homeware, you will enjoy it.

If you are not, there is not much else to do, and an hour spent browsing is an hour you could have spent on the beach.

![The blue painted shopfront of the Burnhams Tea Room and Cafe in Burnham Market](/images/burnham-market-tea-room-shopfront.jpg)

We are not telling you to avoid it, because it is a very good place to sleep and eat. We are telling you not to build your day around it. If you do want to look round properly, go on a weekday morning rather than a Saturday afternoon: it is calmer and the parking is far easier.

## Where to stay

Burnham Market is the right base for this weekend. It sits two miles inland, roughly equidistant from Brancaster and Holkham, and it puts you forty minutes from Sandringham for the Sunday.

**The Hoste** is the obvious choice and it earns it. It is two hotels either side of the road: the original inn, which is dog friendly, and the Georgian Vine House opposite, which is adults only and does not take dogs. We stayed in the Vine House. The room was cosy and spotless, the staff were genuinely lovely, and there is free parking, which in this village is worth more than it sounds. Late summer was around £130 to £140 a night and it is cheaper outside the school holidays.

One thing worth addressing: the Hoste took a run of mediocre reviews a little while ago under different ownership. That is not the place we stayed in, it was certainly an enjoyable stay.

## Where to eat

### The White Horse, Brancaster Staithe

The best meal of the weekend by a distance. Bear in mind that it is mostly seafood. The views across the marsh from the dining room are the sort you stop talking to look at, and the oysters and mussels come from a few hundred metres away, which they will tell you and which you can certainly believe from the taste.

![The painted sign above the entrance to the White Horse restaurant at Brancaster Staithe](/images/the-white-horse-brancaster-staithe-sign.jpg)

It is not cheap. It is also not as expensive as somewhere with that food and that view has any right to be, and it was the thing we talked about on the drive home.

### The Lord Nelson, Burnham Thorpe

Worth the short drive inland on the Friday evening. This is the pub in the village where Nelson was born, and it claims to be the first in England renamed in his honour after the Battle of the Nile. It was derelict by the 1960s, and it closed again for five years before the Holkham Estate bought it and reopened it in 2021 under the arm of Woodforde's brewery, so the building you eat in has been rescued twice.

Good food and a properly considered wine list. The sticky toffee pudding is the thing to order if you have the room. They also serve Nelson's Blood, a rum blend created by a former landlord in the 1970s, named for the story that Nelson's body was preserved in a barrel of spirits on the voyage home from Trafalgar. Neither of us drinks rum, so we cannot tell you if it is any good, only that ordering one is clearly the correct thing to do.

Note that there is a separate pub called The Nelson in Burnham Market. Similar name, different place, no rum.

### Dinner at the Hoste

We ate in on the Saturday and did not regret staying put. The kitchen leans hard on British and where possible Norfolk sourcing, which means the menu shifts through the year, and the current one is always on their site if you want to look before you book. Priced about where you would expect for the village, and the quality justified it.

## A perfect weekend, in order

**Friday evening.** Arrive in Burnham Market, drop bags at the Hoste, then drive ten minutes inland to the Lord Nelson at Burnham Thorpe for dinner. Sticky toffee pudding. Early night, because of what happens next.

**Saturday, first thing.** Brancaster beach at low tide. Check the tide times the night before and set the alarm around them rather than around your preferences. Park on Beach Road, walk out onto the sand, look at the Vina from a safe distance, and enjoy having the place to yourselves.

**Saturday, late morning.** Branodunum and the marsh boardwalk loop, twenty to thirty minutes at an amble.

**Saturday lunch.** The White Horse at Brancaster Staithe. Book it.

**Saturday afternoon.** A wander round Burnham Market if you want it, then back to the hotel. We had a siesta instead and have no regrets.

**Saturday evening.** Dinner at the Hoste, and a quiet glass or two afterwards.

**Sunday morning.** Sandringham. House, museum, then the gardens, with a coffee and a sausage roll from the Stables Cafe when you flag. Do not skip the church of St Mary Magdalene.

**Sunday afternoon.** Castle Rising on the way out, an hour in the keep, then home.

## Pro tips

**Set your weekend by the tide, not the clock.** Low tide is when Brancaster beach is worth the drive, and the times shift by roughly an hour each day. Look them up before you book dinner on the Friday.

**Book Sandringham online and the day before.** It is a few pounds cheaper than the gate, and it locks in your house slot.

**Budget for parking properly.** £6 for two hours at Brancaster beach, plus £5 for the day at Sandringham if you prebook it with your ticket. Neither is enormous, both are annoying if unexpected.

**The Vine House is adults only, the Hoste Inn is dog friendly.** Same hotel, opposite sides of the road, entirely different answer if you are bringing a dog.

**If it is winter, reverse the priorities.** The marsh boardwalk gets wet, Sandringham House is closed from October, and Castle Rising drops to Wednesday to Sunday. The beach is still magnificent and the pubs are still there.
